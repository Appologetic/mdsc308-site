Depression Among LGBTQIA2S+ Students

Depression is a broad term used to describe one of five specific disorders characterized by symptoms of sadness, loss of interest, and feelings of guilt, worthlessness, helplessness, hopelessness, and sadness and, in some cases, symptoms of major depressive disorder (MDD) can even lead to, or include, thoughts of suicide (1-2).

The central nervous system in our bodies uses several key neurotransmitters to function, including serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine. Neurotransmitters such as these are chemical messengers that the cells of our nervous system (our nerves) use to communicate with one another, and the messages they help relay are believed to regulate our mood. However, in cases of depression, there is an imbalance in the levels of these three key neurotransmitters, referred to as the monoamine hypothesis, which causes what you can think of as lousy cell phone reception between the two nerves that are trying to communicate (3-4). Because of this horrible reception, the cells cannot tell each other how to act due to the low levels of neurotransmitters. Specifically, low levels of norepinephrine have been associated with poor memory, lethargy, poor emotional regulation, poor mood, and an inability to concentrate (5). Serotonin and dopamine on the other hand, are commonly called the "feel good" neurotransmitters, and low levels of them have been found to impair signal transduction, the ability of the cell to respond to other neurotransmitters and chemicals in the environment (5). Due to these associated symptoms, the monoamine hypothesis recognizes an imbalance in these neurotransmitters as a probable cause for the symptoms of depression.

This question is primarily one of self-identification. For our purposes, any student that identifies as a member of the LGBTQIA2S+ community is considered a sexual minority. This includes but is not limited to students identifying as lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, non-binary or gender non-conforming, two-spirited, etc.

Students as a whole are already at an incredibly high risk for experiencing depressive symptoms, with over 1/3 of all students globally aged 10-19 years of age experiencing them in 2020 (6), and almost 1/5 students experiencing major depressive disorder (6), an even more severe form of this mental illness. Not only is the prevalence of depression among students high, but it's also climbing and has increased by 13% in the last 10 years (6).

When it comes to students that identify as a sexual minority, the risk is even more significant. Not only are they almost 2x as likely as heterosexual students to cope with moderate to severe depression(7), but they are also 2.74x more likely to be hospitalized and treated for injuries sustained during an attempted suicide (7). Taken together, these statistics are striking and indicate an immediate need for an intervention that specifically helps these students reduce the prevalence and burden of depression among this population and, in doing so, hopefully, save the lives of sexual minority students.

Studies have determined a number of social and societal factors that put students and sexual minorities at greater risk for depression. For students, these include the perceived collective school efficacy, or the trust that an individual has that the school will support them when they are struggling, and the ability to seek support from social systems like friends and family (8). Additionally, victimization, such as traditional forms of bullying, cyberbullying, harassment, the threat of harm, etc., was found to have a damaging effect on these factors, putting students at additional risk for depression (8).

For sexual minorities, victimization through harassment and abuse was also found to be associated with the increased prevalence of depression in this community, in addition to reduced family/social support and increased levels of stress (9). Stress as a contributor to depression is critical as members of this community experience what is known as minority stress (10). Since sexual minorities are stigmatized in our society, they experience more stress than heterosexual individuals based on the expectation of rejection, increased danger of victimization, and, most significantly, the feeling that the world is homophobic (10). The minority stress model tells us that stress processes related to these perceptions can lead to adverse mental health outcomes and has specifically been shown to increase depression among sexual minorities (10).

It's important to note that students of a sexual minority belong to both these groups, which have been demonstrated separately to be specifically at risk, which puts them at even greater risk of experiencing depression. However, the threat they experience is more than just the sum of both groups. Instead, a level of interaction exists between being a member of these two groups that makes the experience of being a student belonging to a sexual minority different from just being a student or just being a member of a sexual minority. This perspective is called intersectionality and is demonstrated wonderfully by the factors contributing to depression for sexual minorities and students. Notice that the lack of social support systems appears as a risk factor for both groups and that victimization, which also appears for both groups, further impairs this (11-13). Health inequities exist where diseases, including mental illnesses like depression, unevenly impact a marginalized population due to risk factors that act through social conditions, which in public health are called the social determinants of health. In the case of sexual minority students, depression impacts this marginalized population to a much greater extent than heterosexual students, as discussed previously, where their gender, sexual orientation and studentship expose them to the mechanism described in the sections above and act as a social determinant of health. As such, depression among this population is a health inequity. Luckily, this means that interventions targeting this population and the societal risk factors that expose them to depression can lessen the burden and positively impact students of a sexual minority.

While on the surface, it may seem like the biological and social causes of depression are separate, they actually interact through stress. As discussed in previous sections, sexual minority students are subject to the minority stress model in which their status as a sexual minority student exposes them to stress that heterosexual students would not face. This can lead to chronic or over-activated stress responses in the nervous system, which can be harmful and lead to an increased risk of depression through reduced neurotransmitter activity, as per the monoamine hypothesis (14).
